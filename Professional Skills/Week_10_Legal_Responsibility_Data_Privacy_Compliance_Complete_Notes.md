## Page 08 — Ethical Decision-Making Models — Utilitarianism

### Original Slide Content

Ethical Decision-Making Models
 1. Utilitarianism (Outcome-Based)
 “Choose the option that benefits the most people.” - Used for large-scale decisions (e.g., pandemic
 tracking apps). - Problem: Sometimes harms a small group unfairly.
 Example 1 - Pandemic Tracking App
 • Scenario:
    A government launches a mobile app to track COVID-19 exposure.
    The app collects location + health data from millions of people.
 • Benefits (Majority):
 • Saves lives by notifying exposed individuals
 • Helps healthcare systems prepare
 • Reduces spread of infection
 • Harms (Minority):
 • Some users lose privacy
 • Sensitive health data could be misused if leaked
 Utilitarian Conclusion:
 Deploy the app because the benefit to society outweighs privacy risks, but with safeguards (encryption,
 consent, transparency).

### Visual Explanation

Utilitarianism is a benefit-vs-harm balance.

```text
Pandemic Tracking App
├── Benefits to majority
│   ├── Saves lives
│   ├── Notifies exposed people
│   ├── Helps healthcare systems
│   └── Reduces spread
└── Harms to minority
    ├── Privacy loss
    └── Sensitive data misuse risk

Decision: deploy only with encryption + consent + transparency.
```

### Drawn Comparison Table — Ethical Models
| Model | Main Question | Strength | Weakness | Best Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Utilitarianism | Which option benefits most people? | Good for public-scale decisions | Can harm minorities | Pandemic app, traffic AI |
| Deontology | Does the action violate a duty/right? | Protects privacy, honesty, consent | Can be rigid | Employee monitoring, dark patterns |
| Stakeholder Analysis | Who is affected and how? | Includes vulnerable groups | Needs careful analysis | AI hiring, accessibility |

### Explanation
Utilitarianism is outcome-based: choose the option with the greatest overall benefit, but reduce harm to affected minorities.

### Exam Tip
Exam focus: explain utilitarianism with majority benefit, minority harm, and safeguards.

### Common Mistake
Do not write “majority benefit” only. Always mention safeguards for the harmed minority.

## Page 10 — Ethical Decision-Making Models — Deontology

### Original Slide Content

Ethical Decision-Making Models
 2. Deontology (Duty-Based)
 “Some actions are always right or wrong.” - Violating privacy is wrong
 even if it benefits society. - Useful for identifying non-negotiable rules.

 Example 1 - Employee Data Privacy
 • Scenario:
   A company wants to secretly monitor employee messages to improve
   productivity.
 • Utilitarian View:
   “Better productivity benefits the company and customers.”
 • Deontological View:
   Secret monitoring violates employee rights, breaks trust, and ignores
   consent. Therefore, it is always wrong, even if it boosts productivity.

### Visual Explanation

Deontology checks the action itself.

```text
Action proposed
     ↓
Does it violate privacy / consent / honesty?
     ↓
If yes: unethical even if outcome is profitable or efficient
```

### Explanation
Deontology is duty-based: privacy, consent, honesty, and autonomy should not be violated even when the outcome looks beneficial.

### Exam Tip
Exam focus: explain deontology using privacy, consent, honesty, and user autonomy.

### Common Mistake
Do not judge deontology by profit or outcome; judge whether the action violates a duty/right.

## Page 12 — Ethical Decision-Making Models — Stakeholder Analysis

### Original Slide Content

Ethical Decision-Making Models
 3. Stakeholder Analysis
 “Consider everyone affected by the decision.” - Includes users, non-users, employees, vulnerable
 groups. - Belonging emphasizes including underrepresented voices.
 Example 1 - AI Hiring System
 Scenario:
 A company adopts AI to filter job applications.
 Stakeholders:
 • Job applicants, HR team, Company leadership, Underrepresented groups (women, minorities),
   Society (workplace equality)
 Analysis:
 • The algorithm might unintentionally favor certain groups, excluding others.
   This harms diversity and violates fairness.
 Stakeholder Decision:
 • Test for bias
 • Use diverse datasets
 • Maintain human oversight
 • Ensure transparency

### Visual Explanation

Stakeholder analysis map.

```text
AI Hiring System
├── Job applicants
├── HR team
├── Company leadership
├── Women / minorities / underrepresented groups
└── Society

Risks: bias + exclusion + low transparency
Controls: bias testing + diverse datasets + human oversight + transparency
```

### Explanation
Stakeholder analysis forces you to identify every affected group, including vulnerable and underrepresented users.

### Exam Tip
Exam focus: identify stakeholders and propose fair mitigations.

### Common Mistake
Do not ignore vulnerable users or underrepresented groups.

## Page 20 — Sri Lankan Laws — PDPA and Other Acts

### Original Slide Content

IT Laws & Data Protection (Global and Sri Lankan)
 Sri Lankan Laws
 • Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) No. 9 of 2022
 Key points:
 - Consent is required before collecting data.
 - Users can request correction or deletion.
 - Organizations must secure personal data.
 - A Data Protection Authority will enforce rules.

 Other Important Sri Lankan Acts
 • Computer Crimes Act (2007): Criminal offenses related to hacking.
 • Electronic Transactions Act (2006): Legitimizes digital signatures.
 • Right to Information Act (2016): Citizens can request public data.

# Full Lecture Summary

This lecture explains legal responsibility, data privacy, compliance, and ethical practice in IT. Modern technologies such as social media, AI, cloud computing, and mobile apps influence communication, work, voting, shopping, and daily life. Because IT systems affect many people, IT professionals must understand ethics, legal duties, privacy controls, and accountability.

Ethics in IT means using guidelines to decide right and wrong when designing software, handling data, building algorithms, and managing systems. Professional responsibilities apply to clients, employers, colleagues, and society. Ethical decision-making models include utilitarianism, deontology, and stakeholder analysis.

The lecture covers GDPR, CCPA, and Sri Lankan laws such as the Personal Data Protection Act No. 9 of 2022, Computer Crimes Act 2007, Electronic Transactions Act 2006, and Right to Information Act 2016. It explains GDPR user rights, GDPR fines, legal responsibilities, privacy principles, and compliance requirements such as DPIAs, data-processing logs, and cybersecurity controls.

The case studies show real ethical/legal failures: Cambridge Analytica, AI hiring bias, and a Sri Lankan telecom data leak. Good case-study answers must explain what happened, identify ethical issues, apply ethical models, and propose mitigation. Corporate accountability is maintained through audits, Data Protection Officers, documentation, and whistleblowing channels.

# Likely Exam Questions and Short Answers

## Question 1
Define ethics in IT.

**Expected Answer:** Ethics in IT refers to guidelines for deciding right and wrong when designing software, handling data, creating algorithms, and managing systems.

## Question 2
Why are ethics important for IT students?

**Expected Answer:** They may build systems that store personal data, control finances, automate decisions, or influence elections. Poor ethics can harm users and create legal responsibility.

## Question 3
Explain utilitarianism with an IT example.

**Expected Answer:** Utilitarianism chooses the option that benefits the most people. A pandemic tracking app may be accepted if it saves lives and includes safeguards.

## Question 4
What is deontology?

**Expected Answer:** Deontology is duty-based ethics. Some actions, such as secret monitoring or privacy violation, are wrong even if outcomes are beneficial.

## Question 5
What is stakeholder analysis?

**Expected Answer:** Stakeholder analysis considers everyone affected by a decision, including vulnerable and underrepresented groups.

## Question 6
List GDPR user rights mentioned.

**Expected Answer:** Access, correction/rectification, deletion/right to be forgotten, and data portability.

## Question 7
What is the GDPR fine rule?

**Expected Answer:** Up to €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue, whichever is higher.

## Question 8
What is PDPA No. 9 of 2022?

**Expected Answer:** Sri Lanka’s personal data protection law requiring consent, correction/deletion rights, secure personal data, and enforcement by a Data Protection Authority.

## Question 9
What are privacy principles?

**Expected Answer:** Collect only necessary data, use it only for stated purpose, delete it when no longer needed, and keep users informed.

## Question 10
What happened in Cambridge Analytica?

**Expected Answer:** A third-party app harvested data of 87 million Facebook users without proper consent and used it to influence political campaigns.

## Question 11
How can AI hiring bias be reduced?

**Expected Answer:** Use diverse datasets, regular bias testing, diverse stakeholder input, transparency, and human oversight.

## Question 12
What should happen after a data breach?

**Expected Answer:** Patch immediately, notify affected parties, conduct security audits, train staff, and implement encryption/access controls.

## Question 13
Why does accountability matter?

**Expected Answer:** It prevents misuse, encourages transparency, maintains public trust, and proves compliance.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Writing ethics as only personal opinion.
- Using utilitarianism as majority wins without minority safeguards.
- Confusing deontology with outcome-based reasoning.
- Ignoring vulnerable groups in stakeholder analysis.
- Assuming GDPR applies only to EU companies.
- Forgetting GDPR rights: access, correction, deletion, portability.
- Forgetting GDPR fine says whichever is higher.
- Confusing PDPA with Computer Crimes Act.
- Saying compliance means only having a policy.
- Answering case studies without solutions and ethical model application.
- Blaming only the AI model for bias instead of also mentioning biased data and weak testing.
- Treating encryption as the only privacy control.
