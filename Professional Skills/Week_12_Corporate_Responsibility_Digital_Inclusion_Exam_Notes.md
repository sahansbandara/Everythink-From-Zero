### Original Slide Content

Archie Carroll’s Four Responsibilities of Business

- Archie B. Carroll has served on the faculty of the Terry College of Business, University of Georgia since 1972

### Visual Explanation

The slide shows Archie Carroll’s CSR pyramid and a portrait of Archie B. Carroll. The pyramid shows four layers of business responsibility: Economic at the base, then Legal, Ethical, and Philanthropic/Discretionary at the top.

### Visual/Text Diagram

```text
Archie Carroll's CSR Pyramid

                 ┌────────────────────────────┐
                 │ Philanthropic /            │
                 │ Discretionary              │
                 │ Be a good corporate citizen│
                 └──────────────┬─────────────┘
                 ┌──────────────▼─────────────┐
                 │ Ethical                     │
                 │ Do what is right and fair  │
                 └──────────────┬─────────────┘
                 ┌──────────────▼─────────────┐
                 │ Legal                       │
                 │ Obey the law               │
                 └──────────────┬─────────────┘
                 ┌──────────────▼─────────────┐
                 │ Economic                    │
                 │ Be profitable / create value│
                 └────────────────────────────┘
```

### Explanation

Carroll’s model is a famous way to explain corporate social responsibility. It shows that businesses have layered responsibilities. A business must create economic value, obey the law, behave ethically, and voluntarily contribute to society.

### Comparison Table — Carroll’s Four Responsibilities

| Level | Responsibility | Core idea | Simple example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | Philanthropic / Discretionary | Be a good corporate citizen | Community project, day care center |
| 3 | Ethical | Do what is right, just, and fair | Avoid harmful or biased technology |
| 2 | Legal | Obey the law | Follow data protection and employment laws |
| 1 | Economic | Be profitable and create value | Produce valuable goods/services |

### Exam Tip

Very likely exam topic: draw or explain Carroll’s four responsibilities pyramid.

### Common Mistake

Drawing the pyramid in the wrong order. Economic is the base; discretionary/philanthropic is the top.

## Page 04 — Archie Carroll’s Four Responsibilities of Business — Economic and Legal

### Original Slide Content

Archie Carroll’s Four Responsibilities of Business

1. Economic responsibilities
   - produce goods and services of value to society so that the firm may repay its creditors and increase the wealth of its shareholders

2. Legal responsibilities
   - defined by governments in laws that management is expected to obey

### Visual Explanation

No major standalone visual. The slide defines the first two Carroll responsibilities: economic and legal.

### Explanation

Economic responsibility is the base because a business must survive and create value. Legal responsibility means the business must obey rules created by governments. A profitable but illegal business is not responsible.

### Exam Tip

Know economic vs legal responsibility with examples.

### Common Mistake

Saying legal responsibility is optional. It is mandatory.

## Page 05 — Archie Carroll’s Four Responsibilities of Business — Ethical and Discretionary

### Original Slide Content

Archie Carroll’s Four Responsibilities of Business

3. Ethical responsibilities
   - follow the generally held beliefs about behavior in a society

4. Discretionary responsibilities
   - purely voluntary obligations a corporation assumes (Example: Day care center)

### Visual Explanation

No major standalone visual. The slide defines ethical and discretionary responsibilities.

### Explanation

Ethical responsibility is about doing what society considers right, even when the law does not force it. Discretionary responsibility is voluntary social contribution, such as supporting a day care center or community project.

### Exam Tip

Know ethical vs discretionary responsibility. Do not mix voluntary actions with legal duties.

### Common Mistake

Confusing ethical and discretionary responsibility. Ethical = expected right behaviour; discretionary = voluntary contribution.

## Page 07 — Why Corporate Responsibility & Digital Inclusion Matter

### Original Slide Content

Why Corporate Responsibility & Digital Inclusion Matter

- Technology affects every part of modern life, from education and healthcare to transportation, banking, government services, and social interaction.
- Because corporations design and control most digital systems, their decisions influence who gets access, who gets left behind, and who may be harmed unintentionally.
- Corporate responsibility ensures that organisations consider the social and ethical consequences of the technologies they create.
- Digital inclusion ensures that all individuals, regardless of disability, language, income level, or geographic location, can participate meaningfully in the digital world.

### Visual Explanation

No major standalone visual beyond text. The slide explains why corporate decisions in technology affect access, exclusion, and possible harm.

### Visual/Text Diagram

```text
Technology decisions by corporations
        ↓
Digital systems used by society
        ↓
Affects access, fairness, opportunity, and harm
        ↓
Corporate responsibility + digital inclusion
        ↓
More ethical and inclusive digital world
```

### Explanation

IT systems now control access to education, banking, healthcare, government services, and communication. Bad design can exclude users. Responsible IT means thinking about social impact before deploying technology.

### Exam Tip

Use keywords: access, exclusion, harm, ethical consequences, digital systems.

### Common Mistake

Thinking digital systems are neutral. Design decisions can include or exclude people.

## Page 09 — Understanding Digital Inclusion

### Original Slide Content

Understanding Digital Inclusion

- Digital inclusion is not just about providing internet access. It is about ensuring that everyone, especially vulnerable groups, can meaningfully use technology.
- This includes:
  - Skills: Can they use online banking, mobile apps, or learning systems?
  - Support: Are systems available in their language? Is help available?
  - Accessibility: Can people with disabilities use the platforms effectively?

In Sri Lanka, the digital divide is visible between urban vs rural areas, English vs Sinhala/Tamil speakers, and younger vs older populations.

Definition: Ensuring everyone, regardless of age, gender, income, disability, or location, can access and effectively use digital technologies.

### Visual Explanation

No major standalone visual beyond text. The slide explains digital inclusion through skills, support, and accessibility, then gives Sri Lankan divide examples.

### Visual/Text Diagram

```text
Digital Inclusion
├── Access: devices, internet, connectivity
├── Skills: ability to use digital systems
├── Support: language/help/training availability
├── Accessibility: usable by people with disabilities
└── Meaningful use: people can actually benefit from technology
```

### Explanation

Digital inclusion means real usable access, not just internet connection. A person may have internet but still be excluded if they cannot understand the language, cannot afford a device, cannot use the system, or the system is inaccessible.

### Table — Digital Inclusion Is More Than Internet Access

| Area | Question to ask | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Skills | Can users operate the system? | Online banking, learning systems, mobile apps |
| Support | Can users get help in a usable way? | Sinhala/Tamil help, user support |
| Accessibility | Can disabled users use the system? | Screen-reader support, keyboard navigation |
| Meaningful use | Can users actually benefit? | Completing government or banking tasks online |

### Exam Tip

Definition question likely: define digital inclusion and explain beyond internet access.

### Common Mistake

Defining digital inclusion as only internet access. That is too weak.

## Page 10 — Understanding Digital Inclusion — Components and Issues

### Original Slide Content

Understanding Digital Inclusion

Components of Digital Inclusion
- Access: availability of internet, devices, connectivity.
- Affordability: cost barriers.
- Ability: digital literacy and skills.
- Accessibility: inclusive design for disabilities.
- Awareness: understanding benefits of digital technologies.

Global Issues
- 2.7 billion people still offline.
- Rural communities lack high-speed connectivity.
- People with disabilities often left out of tech design.

Sri Lankan Issues
- Rural areas have lower internet and device penetration.
- Some government services lack accessibility tools.
- Digital literacy gaps among senior citizens.

### Visual Explanation

No major standalone visual beyond text. The slide lists components of digital inclusion and separates global issues from Sri Lankan issues.

### Visual/Text Diagram

```text
Digital Inclusion Components
Access → Affordability → Ability → Accessibility → Awareness
  │           │             │             │              │
Internet   Low cost     Skills       Disability     Understand
devices    devices      literacy     inclusion      benefits
```

### Explanation

The five components work together. Access alone is weak. If a user has a phone but cannot afford data, lacks digital skills, or the app is not accessible, digital inclusion has failed.

### Table — Digital Inclusion Components

| Component | Meaning | Failure example |
|---|---|---|
| Access | Internet, devices, connectivity are available | Rural area has weak internet |
| Affordability | People can pay for devices/data | Mobile data is too expensive |
| Ability | Users have digital literacy | Senior citizens cannot use online forms |
| Accessibility | Systems support disabilities | No screen-reader labels |
| Awareness | People know benefits and services | Citizens do not know a service exists |

### Exam Tip

Memorize the 5A model: Access, Affordability, Ability, Accessibility, Awareness.

### Common Mistake

Forgetting affordability and ability. A person may have access but still be excluded.

# Key Definitions

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) | Business responsibility to improve conditions for employees, shareholders, communities, and the environment. | A company designs accessible digital services and supports community education. |
| Economic responsibility | Responsibility to produce goods/services of value and remain financially viable. | A software company creates useful products and earns revenue. |
| Legal responsibility | Responsibility to obey laws and regulations. | Following privacy, labour, and data protection laws. |
| Ethical responsibility | Responsibility to follow generally accepted beliefs about right behaviour in society. | Avoiding biased algorithms even if not legally punished. |
| Discretionary responsibility | Voluntary responsibility a corporation assumes beyond legal/ethical duties. | Funding a day care center or digital literacy programme. |
| Digital inclusion | Ensuring everyone can access and effectively use digital technologies regardless of age, gender, income, disability, or location. | A government portal available in Sinhala, Tamil, and English with accessibility support. |
| Digital divide | The gap between people who can meaningfully access and use digital technology and those who cannot. | Urban users have fast internet while rural users lack stable connectivity. |
| Accessibility | Designing technology so people with disabilities can use it effectively. | Screen-reader support and keyboard navigation. |
| Affordability | Removing cost barriers to digital participation. | Low-cost data plans or public internet access. |
| Digital literacy | Ability to use digital tools and online systems effectively. | Knowing how to use online banking safely. |
| Transparency | Clearly explaining how systems use data and make decisions. | A service explains what personal data it collects and why. |
| Algorithmic bias | Unfair outcomes produced by automated systems due to biased data or design. | A hiring algorithm unfairly rejects a certain group. |

# Likely Exam Questions and Short Answers

## Question 1
Define corporate social responsibility.

**Expected Answer:** Corporate social responsibility means businesses improve conditions for employees, shareholders, communities, and the environment while considering social and ethical consequences.

## Question 2
Explain Archie Carroll’s four responsibilities of business.

**Expected Answer:** The four responsibilities are economic, legal, ethical, and discretionary/philanthropic. Economic means creating value and profit, legal means obeying law, ethical means doing what is fair, and discretionary means voluntary social contribution.

## Question 3
Draw Carroll’s CSR pyramid.

**Expected Answer:** Draw Economic at the bottom, Legal above it, Ethical above legal, and Philanthropic/Discretionary at the top.

## Question 4
Differentiate ethical and legal responsibility.

**Expected Answer:** Legal responsibility is required by law. Ethical responsibility is expected by society even when not directly enforced by law.

## Question 5
What is digital inclusion?

**Expected Answer:** Digital inclusion ensures everyone, regardless of age, gender, income, disability, or location, can access and effectively use digital technologies.

## Question 6
Why is digital inclusion not only internet access?

**Expected Answer:** Because users also need affordable devices/data, digital skills, language support, accessibility tools, and awareness to use technology meaningfully.

## Question 7
List the components of digital inclusion.

**Expected Answer:** Access, affordability, ability, accessibility, and awareness.

## Question 8
Explain why digital inclusion matters in Sri Lanka.

**Expected Answer:** Sri Lanka has digital gaps between urban and rural users, English and Sinhala/Tamil speakers, young and older populations, people with disabilities, and different income groups.

## Question 9
What responsibilities do IT corporations have toward clients?

**Expected Answer:** They must deliver secure, reliable, accessible, inclusive systems, protect user data, provide easy interfaces, and be transparent in data usage.

## Question 10
What responsibilities do employees have toward employers?

**Expected Answer:** They must follow professional codes, internal security measures, cybersecurity policies, privacy policies, report vulnerabilities, and avoid misusing company data.

## Question 11
What responsibilities do corporations have toward society?

**Expected Answer:** They must avoid biased algorithms, misleading services, and systems that worsen inequality, while reducing digital divides and supporting fairness, accessibility, and sustainability.

## Question 12
Give one example of discretionary responsibility.

**Expected Answer:** A company voluntarily runs a community digital literacy programme or supports a day care center.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Writing CSR = charity only. Wrong: CSR includes economic, legal, ethical, and discretionary responsibility.
- Drawing Carroll’s pyramid in the wrong order.
- Confusing ethical responsibility with legal responsibility.
- Defining digital inclusion as only providing internet access.
- Forgetting affordability, ability, accessibility, and awareness.
- Giving answers without Sri Lankan examples when the question asks for local context.
- Talking only about clients and forgetting employers, colleagues, and society.
- Not mentioning data protection, accessibility, inclusiveness, and transparency in IT responsibility answers.
- Thinking technology is neutral. Corporate design decisions can exclude or harm people.
