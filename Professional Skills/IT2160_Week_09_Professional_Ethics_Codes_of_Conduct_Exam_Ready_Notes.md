## Page 04 — Terms

### Original Slide Content

- **Profession:** A calling requiring specialized knowledge and often long and intensive academic preparation.
- **Professional:** A person who engaged in one of the learned professions conforming to the technical or ethical standards of a profession.
- **Professionalism:** The conduct, aims, or qualities that characterize or mark a profession or a professional person.

### Visual Explanation

No major visual content. The slide presents three important definitions.

### Explanation

These three terms are closely connected but not the same:

| Term | Main Meaning | Simple Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Profession | Field requiring special knowledge and academic preparation | A skilled field like medicine, engineering, IT, law |
| Professional | A person who works according to technical and ethical standards | A person who does the job with competence and ethics |
| Professionalism | The behavior and qualities expected from a professional | Acting responsibly, honestly, and competently |

### Visual/Text Diagram

```text
Profession
  └── A specialized field
        ↓
Professional
  └── A person working in that field
        ↓
Professionalism
  └── The expected behavior and quality of that person
```

### Exam Tip

If asked to differentiate these terms, do not write the same meaning for all three. Use separate examples.

### Common Mistake

Calling anyone with a job a professional. A professional must meet technical and ethical standards.

## Page 05 — Who is a Professional?

### Original Slide Content

- Who possesses certain attributes such as:
  - Expert Knowledge
  - Autonomy with respect to “independence in conducting one’s professional practice”
- Have special responsibilities or moral obligations that exceed those of ordinary individuals.

### Visual Explanation

The slide includes an image of different professionals, such as engineers, doctors, business people, and other skilled workers. The purpose is to show that professionalism exists across many fields, not only IT.

### Explanation

A professional is someone with:

1. **Expert knowledge** — they know more than an ordinary person in a specialized area.
2. **Autonomy** — they can make independent professional decisions.
3. **Special moral responsibility** — their work can affect other people, so they must act carefully.

For computing professionals, this means a software engineer cannot say “I only wrote code.” If the code harms users, leaks private data, or creates danger, the engineer has responsibility.

### Visual/Text Diagram

```text
Professional
├── Expert Knowledge
│   └── Specialized skill and training
├── Autonomy
│   └── Independent professional judgement
└── Moral Responsibility
    └── Duties beyond ordinary individuals
```

### Exam Tip

A strong answer should include all three keywords: **expert knowledge**, **autonomy**, and **special responsibility/moral obligation**.

### Common Mistake

Thinking professional means only “paid worker.” Payment alone does not make someone professional.

**Amateur** — a person who takes part in an activity because they are interested in it or enjoy it, not as a job.

Writing “amateur = bad.” That is not always true. The correct difference is that professionals have formal responsibilities and standards.

## Page 12 — Ethics

### Original Slide Content

- Ethics is a set of beliefs about right or wrong behavior.
- Ethical behavior conforms to generally accepted social norms -> Universally accepted.
- Eg: Lying and cheating are unethical.

### Visual Explanation

No major visual content. The slide defines ethics.

### Explanation

Ethics helps decide whether behavior is right or wrong. In professional work, ethics is not only about personal morals. It includes accepted professional and social standards.

### Definition Block

#### Definition
Ethics is a set of beliefs about right and wrong behavior.

#### Simple Meaning
Ethics tells us what we should and should not do.

#### Example
Lying about software testing results is unethical.

### Exam Tip

Use simple examples like **lying, cheating, hiding defects, privacy violation, plagiarism, and bribery**.

### Common Mistake

Writing ethics as “law.” Law and ethics overlap, but they are not identical. Some actions may be legal but still unethical.

# Full Lecture Summary

This lecture explains professional ethics and codes of conduct for IT and computing professionals. It begins by defining **profession**, **professional**, and **professionalism**. A profession requires specialized knowledge and academic preparation. A professional follows technical and ethical standards. Professionalism is the behavior and qualities expected from a professional.

A professional is expected to have expert knowledge, autonomy, and special moral responsibilities. In computing, professionals include software engineers, database administrators, system analysts, software managers, technical writers, hardware designers, and computer scientists.

The lecture emphasizes that computing work can have serious real-world impact. Software systems may be used in aircraft, medical treatment, transportation, nuclear reactors, missile systems, building design, and healthcare models. Because these systems can affect life and public safety, computing professionals must act ethically.

Ethics is a set of beliefs about right and wrong behavior. Professional codes of ethics guide professionals in difficult situations. They help professionals act honestly, avoid harm, protect privacy, maintain competence, respect intellectual property, reject bribery, and prioritize public welfare.

The lecture introduces professional codes from CSSL, IEEE, ACM, IET, IEEE-CS, and BCS. CSSL focuses on professionalism, professional development, added value to Sri Lanka, honesty, and competence. IEEE emphasizes safety, welfare, honesty, conflict avoidance, and rejecting bribery. ACM emphasizes society, human well-being, avoiding harm, trustworthiness, fairness, intellectual property, privacy, and confidentiality.

The ACM Software Engineering Code adds eight principles: public, client and employer, product, judgement, management, profession, colleagues, and self. A key idea is that client and employer interests must be consistent with public interest.

The lecture ends with practical ethical scenarios: an ATM fault and a cancer-treatment device project. In both cases, the ethical solution is not to hide risks or prioritize deadlines over safety. The professional response is to report issues, document risks, continue testing, fix problems, and protect users and the public.

# Likely Exam Questions and Short Answers

## Question 1
Define profession, professional, and professionalism.

**Expected Answer:**  
A profession is a calling requiring specialized knowledge and often intensive academic preparation. A professional is a person who works in a learned profession while following technical and ethical standards. Professionalism is the conduct, aims, and qualities expected from a professional person.

## Question 2
Who is a professional?

**Expected Answer:**  
A professional is a person with expert knowledge, autonomy in professional practice, and special responsibilities or moral obligations that exceed those of ordinary individuals.

## Question 3
Who is a computing professional?

**Expected Answer:**  
A computing professional is anyone employed in computer, IT, or information/communication fields. Examples include software engineers, hardware designers, database administrators, system analysts, software managers, technical writers, and computer scientists.

## Question 4
Why is ethics important for IT professionals?

**Expected Answer:**  
Ethics helps IT professionals make moral decisions, act responsibly, avoid harm, protect users, and consider the impact of computing systems on society. This is important because computing systems can affect safety, privacy, finance, healthcare, and public welfare.

## Question 5
List responsibilities of computing professionals.

**Expected Answer:**  
Computing professionals have responsibilities to clients and users, employers, other professionals, and the public.

## Question 6
List five purposes of professional codes of ethics.

**Expected Answer:**  
The five purposes are to inspire, educate, guide, define responsibilities, and discipline members.

## Question 7
State five CSSL codes of ethics.

**Expected Answer:**  
Professionalism, professional development, added value to Sri Lanka, honesty, and competence.

## Question 8
State selected IEEE ethical principles.

**Expected Answer:**  
Accept responsibility for safety and welfare, avoid conflicts of interest, be honest, and reject bribery in all forms.

## Question 9
State important ACM code principles.

**Expected Answer:**  
Contribute to society and human well-being, avoid harm, be honest and trustworthy, be fair and non-discriminatory, respect intellectual property, give credit, respect privacy, and honour confidentiality.

## Question 10
What should a programmer do if they find a fault in ATM software?

**Expected Answer:**  
The programmer should honestly inform the employer, document the issue, help fix and test the program, and protect users from harm. Hiding the fault is unethical and may create worse consequences.

## Question 11
In the cancer-treatment device scenario, should the project manager deliver without testing?

**Expected Answer:**  
No. Since the system is life-critical, patient safety and public interest must come before deadline pressure. The project manager should report the risk, request more time, continue testing, document limitations, and release only after adequate safety assurance.

## Question 12
Why is “public interest” important in the ACM SE-Code?

**Expected Answer:**  
Public interest is important because software can affect society, safety, privacy, and well-being. Software engineers must serve clients and employers only in ways that are consistent with public interest.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Saying professional means only “paid worker.” A professional must follow standards and responsibilities.
- Confusing profession, professional, and professionalism.
- Ignoring public interest in scenario answers.
- Choosing deadline over safety in life-critical systems.
- Writing only one stakeholder in stakeholder analysis.
- Treating ethics as personal opinion only.
- Forgetting privacy and confidentiality.
- Forgetting intellectual property and proper credit.
- Saying employer interest is always highest priority.
- Not mentioning honesty when a software fault is discovered.
- Not discussing testing in medical/software safety scenarios.
- Writing codes of ethics as only “rules” and missing their functions: inspire, educate, guide, responsibilities, discipline.
