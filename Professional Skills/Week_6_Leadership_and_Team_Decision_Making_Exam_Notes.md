# Full Lecture Summary

Week 6 covers two connected topics: **team leadership** and **team decision-making**. The leadership part explains what leadership means, why it is controversial, and how different situations require different leadership approaches. It introduces the trait perspective, contingency/situational perspective, and transformational perspective. The decision-making part explains decisions as considered choices, then shows how teams can make better decisions through systematic processes, healthy thinking environments, suitable methods, and practical tools such as brainstorming, affinity grouping, multi-voting, criteria matrices, and Six Thinking Hats. The final visual links decision structures to information systems: structured decisions with TPS, semi-structured decisions with MIS, and unstructured decisions with DSS.

| Term | Meaning | Example / Use |
|---|---|---|
| Leadership | Knowing yourself, communicating vision, building trust, and taking effective action. | A team leader aligns members around a project goal. |

| Decision | A judgment, opinion, or position reached after consideration. | Choosing which project idea to implement. |
| Decision-making | Cognitive process of choosing between alternatives using thinking, memory, and evaluation. | Team compares alternatives before choosing. |
| Thinking Environment | A team environment that improves quality of thinking and contribution. | Attention, equality, appreciation, diversity. |

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Writing that leadership is only giving orders — incorrect; leadership includes vision, trust, self-awareness, and action.
- Ignoring followers — leadership depends on follower inclusion, initiative, cooperation, readiness, ability, and willingness.
- Treating Great Man Theory as a modern complete explanation — it is an early and biased view.
- Confusing trait theory with situational theory — trait theory focuses on leader qualities; situational theory focuses on context and follower readiness.
- Using the same leadership style for every follower — weak answer; style must match readiness.
- Confusing decision with decision-making — decision is the final choice; decision-making is the process.
- Brainstorming and judging ideas at the same time — brainstorming should first generate ideas.
- Calling affinity grouping a voting method — it groups ideas, not votes on them.
- Using majority vote when consensus is needed for high commitment.
- Using authority without discussion for complex decisions that need team buy-in.
- Ignoring weights in a criteria matrix.
- Mixing DSS, MIS, and TPS roles in the information systems pyramid.
