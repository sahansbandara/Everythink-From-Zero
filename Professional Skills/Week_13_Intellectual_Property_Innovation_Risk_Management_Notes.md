## Page 07 — Intellectual Property

### Original Slide Content
- **Intellectual property (IP):** Any intangible asset that consists of human knowledge and ideas.
- Examples:
  - patents
  - copyrights
  - trade secrets
  - trademarks
  - contracts
- Intent:
  - to encourage ingenuity
  - to protect creative ventures
- “Value” of an intellectual asset:
  - Most internally generated IP assets cannot be recognized on a balance sheet because it is difficult to objectively value IP assets.
  - They can be included in a balance sheet if acquired, which allows more accurate valuation using acquisition cost.

### Visual Explanation
No major visual content.

### Explanation
IP is not a physical item. It is the value attached to an idea, invention, work, brand, or secret business information. For example, the codebase of an application, a logo, and an algorithm can all have IP value.

### Comparison Table

| Concept | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Tangible asset | Physical property | laptop, building, machine |
| Intangible asset | Non-physical value | software, brand, patent, copyright |
| IP asset | Human idea/knowledge protected by law | invention, source code, trademark |

### Exam Tip
Define IP as **intangible asset based on human knowledge and ideas** and give at least two examples.

### Common Mistake
Thinking IP must always appear on a balance sheet. Internally generated IP may be hard to value objectively.

## Page 08 — IP Confusion Cartoon

### Original Slide Content
The cartoon text says:

> “All I asked was, ‘Can I patent my copyrighted trademark?!!’”

### Visual Explanation
The cartoon jokes about confusing different IP categories:
- **Patent** protects inventions.
- **Copyright** protects expression of ideas.
- **Trademark** protects brand identifiers.

### Explanation
This slide warns students not to mix IP types. One creation can involve multiple IP protections, but the protections are not the same.

### Visual Comparison Table

| Wrong Mixed Term | Why It Is Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|---|
| “Patent my copyrighted trademark” | Patent, copyright, and trademark protect different things | Decide what is being protected first |

| Copyright | Not for ideas alone | For fixed expression |

### Exam Tip
If a question asks “which IP type applies?”, identify the object first:
- invention → patent,
- logo/name → trademark,
- book/code/art → copyright,
- confidential business info → trade secret.

### Common Mistake
Using “patent,” “copyright,” and “trademark” interchangeably.

A legal certificate that gives you the right to:
- Protect your invention
- For 20 years
- In specific territories

Additional visual labels:
- State or by a regional office acting for several states
- Exclusive right — prevent or stop others from commercially exploiting

### Visual Explanation

### Recreated Diagram


- 20 years,
- specific territories.

Saying a patent gives worldwide protection automatically. It does not.

# Full Lecture Summary

This lecture explains intellectual property, innovation, and risk management in computing. Intellectual property refers to intangible creations of the human mind, such as inventions, software code, literary works, logos, commercial symbols, designs, trade secrets, and copyrighted works.

The lecture begins by explaining why IP matters in computing. Digital technology can create major value, but it can also cause harm through piracy, code theft, biased systems, privacy breaches, and cybersecurity failures. Therefore, ethical IT practice requires respecting ownership, avoiding plagiarism and piracy, and considering the consequences of technology.

The lecture explains major IP types: patents, trademarks, geographical indications, industrial designs, copyright, related rights, and trade secrets. It then explains Sri Lanka’s IP system, especially the Intellectual Property Act No. 36 of 2003, which covers copyrights, patents, industrial designs, trademarks, unfair competition, geographical information, undisclosed information, and layout designs.

A major part of the lecture focuses on patents. A patent is a legal certificate that gives exclusive rights to protect an invention, usually for 20 years from filing date, in specific territories. Patentability requires novelty, utility/industrial applicability, and non-obviousness/inventive step. Patent filing can follow national, regional, or international/PCT routes. Patent strategy must consider why, what, who, when, and where to file.

The lecture also explains licensing, software piracy, fair use, open-source software, trademarks, copyright, proprietary licenses, reverse engineering, and trade secrets. Finally, it introduces IT risk management, including cybersecurity risks, legal/IP risks, ethical risks, and operational risks. The risk management process is Identify → Analyse → Mitigate → Monitor.

# Likely Exam Questions and Short Answers

## Question 1
What is intellectual property?

**Expected Answer:**  
Intellectual property is an intangible asset consisting of human knowledge and ideas. It includes patents, copyrights, trademarks, trade secrets, industrial designs, and other rights related to creative or intellectual activity.

## Question 2
Differentiate patent, copyright, and trademark.

**Expected Answer:**  
A patent protects inventions such as processes, machines, or products. Copyright protects the expression of ideas, such as software code or books. A trademark protects brand identifiers such as names, logos, and symbols used to distinguish goods or services.

---

## Question 3
List the main types of IP protection.

**Expected Answer:**  
Patents, trademarks, geographical indications, industrial designs, copyright, related rights, and trade secrets.

---

## Question 4
What are the three patentability criteria?

**Expected Answer:**  
Novelty, utility/industrial applicability, and non-obviousness/inventive step.

---

## Question 5
What is prior art and why does it matter?

**Expected Answer:**  
Prior art is information that was publicly known before a patent filing or priority date. It matters because it can destroy novelty and prevent a patent from being granted.

---

## Question 6
Explain the first-to-file principle.

**Expected Answer:**  
The first-to-file principle means patent priority is given to the person who files the patent application first. Public disclosure before filing can allow others to file first or can make the invention prior art.

---

## Question 7
What are the patent filing routes?

**Expected Answer:**  
Patent applications can be filed through national routes, regional routes, or international route using the PCT.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Saying IP protects only physical property.
- Confusing patents, copyrights, and trademarks.
- Saying copyright protects ideas. It protects expression of ideas.
- Thinking a patent gives automatic worldwide protection.
- Forgetting patent protection is territorial and time-limited.
- Forgetting the three patentability criteria: novelty, utility, non-obviousness.
- Publicly disclosing an invention before filing and ignoring prior art risk.
- Thinking open-source means “no license.”
- Thinking piracy is acceptable if it is common.
- Confusing software licensing with ownership.
- Thinking trade secrets protect against independent creation.
- Treating risk management as only cybersecurity.
- Stopping risk management after mitigation and forgetting monitoring.
